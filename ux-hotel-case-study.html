Hotel Booking Case Study

Define

Goals

A new hotel company wanted to create a mobile booking app that would allow users to reserve rooms. Its competitors include aggregators like Hotels.com and individual brands like Best Western.

Scope

This project was the focus of a course offered by UX Design Institute. It lasted for six months and covered the full design process from research to wireframes.

Research

The research phase included a competitive benchmark as well as user testing. I began with competitive benchmarking to get an overview of the environment. I then moved on to user testing to see how users fared with competitor sites. I found that even in a crowded market, there was room for improvement even for established brands.

Competitive Benchmark

The competitive benchmarking process mapped the current hotel booking landscape. It revealed established norms and potential trouble spots. It yielded a few key findings:

  • Uncluttered interfaces were helpful
  • Minimizing form length was a positive
  • Encountering frequent sign-up calls to action was frustrating
  • Well-placed trip summaries were helpful
  • Date selection was often confusing

Usability Testing

Insights were collected from three different usability tests. Two of the tests were part of the course materials, while the third was conducted live. This pool was small and did not cover a particularly broad demographic range. It would have been helpful to conduct tests with a few more people to ensure adequate coverage. Despite the small test group, usability testing allowed me to observe which parts of the process were positive and which sparked frustration. Users were quite communicative about what they liked and didn't like about the process:

"...nearly put in the wrong dates"

- Tester 2, on struggles with a competitor's slow-loading calendar

"...puts me in the holiday mood"

- Tester 1, on a video of a swimming tropical fish

"I have my contacts in, but I can't really read this"

- Tester 3, on encountering white mileage text over a light image

Analysis

On my first attempt at an affinity diagram, I created groups based on UI location. After re-working my diagram based on instructor feedback, new patterns emerged. Several things, like iconography and good copy, turned out to be overarching themes. The customer journey map of the users' whole decision process showed that the beginning of the booking process is a high point, but complicated workflows can really bring down the mood.

Design

The design phase involved planning the user's flow through the app as well as sketches of screens based on the insights gained in previous stages.

Flow Diagram

The flow diagram determined the primary user flow through the app. I kept this flow similar to competitors' to follow established conventions. I tried to minimize interactions to keep things simple for the user.

Sketches

Sketches allowed me to quickly iterate over solutions for the issues that surfaced during the research and analysis phases. I paid special attention to issues such as:

  • Added a date selector with a vertical scroll to the next month to keep selection simple
  • Created two large tabs to allow for map and filter views on the results page
  • Kept the checkout form as short as possible
  • Included TripAdvisor reviews widget to add trust

Prototype

I chose to create a medium-fidelity prototype. It provided a close representation of the final product for testing while still having a fairly quick turnaround time. While setting up the prototype, I ended up adding the map to the top of the results page and making the filters a modal. Hopefully having a map being visible first thing will help users pinpoint the location more easily.

Conclusion

This project identified some key areas where a new hotel could outshine existing hotel apps. These areas included the search, calendar, and final booking stages. I tried my best to remove my own biases by allowing the feedback of users to guide the design of the new app.

"Nice prototype the CTA's were obvious. It was intuitive simple to use, well done. It's clear to see the solutions already defined in the flow diagram and interaction design have been depicted here."

Rebecca Bradley, UX Design Institute

Retrospective

  • Seeing someone else use an app was extremely helpful in thinking about improvements. People verbalizing frustration with a glitchy calendar or with low-contrast text were really valuable soundbites.
  • A survey would have added more helpful data and allowed for triangulation.
  • A user test of my working prototype would have provided data on whether moving the map and filters to the top of the screen helped users narrow down their searches faster.

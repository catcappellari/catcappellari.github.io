Hotel Booking Case Study

Intro

Goals

The goal was to create a mobile booking app for a new hotel company that was hoping for a simple and accessible process that would allow users to quickly find and reserve hotel rooms. The competitive landscape includes giant companies like Hotels.com as well as brand-specific sites and apps like Best Western and Wyndham. The large group of competitors meant that usability and convenience were key.

Scope

This project was done through a course offered by UX Design Institute. It lasted for six months and covered the full design process from research to wireframes.

Roles

  • UX Research
  • Analysis
  • UX Design
  • Prototyping
  • Wireframes

Research

The research phase included a competitive benchmark as well as user testing. I began with competitive benchmarking to get an overall feel for the environment, then moved on to user testing to see how users fared with competitor sites. These findings revealed that even in a crowded market, there was room for a new app to provide a better experience than that found with many established brands. Some of the existing hotel app solutions had gotten a bit bogged-down with calls to action and lengthy forms, while others were difficult to operate.

Competitive Benchmark

The competitive benchmarking process was helpful in getting to know the current hotel booking landscape, including established norms and potential trouble spots. It yielded a few key findings:

  • Uncluttered interfaces were helpful
  • Minimizing form length was a positive
  • Encountering frequent sign-up calls to action was frustrating
  • Well-placed trip summaries were helpful
  • Date selection was often a point of confusion

Usability Testing

Insights were collected from three different usability tests. Two of the tests were part of the course materials, while the third was conducted live. The tester demographics were as follows:

  • Participant 1: male, Irish, father of small children, 35 - 45
  • Participant 2: male, Irish, in a long-term relationship, 25 - 35
  • Participant 3: male, American, in a long-term relationship, 25 - 35

This pool was fairly small and did not cover a particularly broad demographic range. It would have been helpful to conduct tests with a few more people to ensure adequate coverage. Hotel apps are used by a large and diverse audience, after all.

Despite the small test group, usability testing still allowed us to get to know the target user base and discover which parts of the process were positive and which sparked frustration. The insights collected included:

  • Videos/imagery help people get excited
  • Calendar selection could be tricky (slow load, unintuitive, etc)
  • Photos and copy important

"...nearly put in the wrong dates"

- Tester 2, on struggles with a competitors slow-loading calendar

"...puts me in the holiday mood"

"I have my contacts in, but I can't really read this"

- Tester 3, on encountering white mileage text over a white image

Analysis

The analysis phase of the design process deals with organizing and synthesizing information gathered in the research phase. Patterns begin to emerge at this stage, and lessons learned must be grouped so that they can be used in the design phase. I began with creating an affinity diagram with insights from the competitive benchmark and the user testing. The affinity diagram helped me zoom in on issues and group them in meaningful ways. After that, I completed a customer journey map of the users' whole decision process. This provided a bird's-eye view of the task.

Affinity Diagram

The affinity diagram stage synthesized the findings from the research stage. On my first attempt at an affinity diagram, I found myself creating groups based on location in the process on the app. After re-working my diagram, new patterns emerged. Several things, like iconography and good copy, were items that were themes throughout the experience and not just local to one part of the process. Seeing this as its own group on the board highlighted how important it was. Key groups included:

  • Reasons for choosing a brand
  • UI elements (the date selector, iconography, etc)
  • Filtering

Customer Journey Map

The customer journey map helped to identify where the product fits within the user's larger decision-making process as well as to identify various emotions through the hotel booking experience. The journey map was helpful in solidifying which parts of the online booking experience went right and where parts of it went wrong. The customer journey map helped really drive home the importance of making sure the search was as good an experience as possible since the user tests had shown that it was a point of frustration. Top insights included:

  • Customers often think and research about the trip before committing to a particular hotel
  • The beginning and end of the process are typically the most enjoyable for users
  • The location search can be frustrating if poorly implemented

Design

The design phase incorporated everything learned in the prior stages and included the process of planning the user's flow through the app and sketches of screens.

Flow Diagram

The flow diagram determined the primary user flow through the app. This was kept similar to competitors' in order to follow established conventions. I paid special attention to reducing interactions as much as possible to keep things simple for the user.

View Full Flow Diagram

Sketches

Sketches of each of the screens in the app serve as a rough draft for the final product with special attention to issues that were noted previously such as:

  • Added a date selector with a vertical scroll to the next month to keep selection as intuitive as possible
  • Created two large tabs to allow for map and filter views on the results page
  • Kept the checkout form as short as possible
  • Included TripAdvisor reviews widget to add trust as this is a new hotel brand

Prototyping

The prototyping stage included a medium-fidelity prototype to provide a close representation of the final product or testing while still having a fairly quick completion time. While setting up the prototype, I ended up adding the map to the top of the results page and making the filters a modal. I realized that a large frustration in the search process was not having a good idea of where each hotel was located, so hopefully a map being visible first thing will be helpful.

Prototype

The prototype was set up to test the following scenario:

  1. Select Baltimore as destination
  2. Set dates to be June 10 - 16
  3. Edit the reservation to be for 2 adults
  4. Add a AAA discount (testing the filters for usability)
  5. Set distance to be up to 5 miles
  6. Set price to be $75 - $125 (testing the price range for usability)
  7. Make sure the breakfast is complimentary
  8. Select a queen room at Hotel on the Inner Harbor
  9. Check out as member

Conclusion

This project identified some key areas where a new hotel could outshine existing hotel apps, including in the search, calendar, and final booking stages. By walking through the design process, I did my best to remove my own biases or expectations from the picture by allowing the feedback of users to guide the design of the new app.

"Nice prototype the CTA's were obvious. It was intuitive simple to use, well done. It's clear to see the solutions already defined in the flow diagram and interaction design have been depicted here."

Rebecca Bradley, UX Design Institute

Observing other people using a product helps remove personal biases as well as highlight areas for improvement. As someone who relies heavily on filters and maps, I was surprised to see that the testers did not quickly reach for either of these tools. I tried to make these tools easier to find on my app in case they just were not obvious enough on the tested apps.

The importance of following accessibility guidelines also came to the forefront when one user mentioned that he was struggling to read low-contrast text. Sound bites like this one could be very helpful in making sure everyone is on board with following best practices.

A survey would have added more helpful data and allowed for triangulation. A user test of my working prototype would have provided data on whether the map and filters being more obvious actually helped users narrow down their searches faster. All in all, I really enjoyed working on this project and look forward to learning more as I complete more projects.

Hotel Booking Case Study

Define

Goals

A new hotel company wanted to create a mobile booking app that would allow users to reserve rooms. Its competitors include aggregators like Hotels.com and individual brands like Best Western.

Scope

This project was the focus of a course offered by UX Design Institute. It lasted for six months and covered the full design process from research to mockups.

Roles

  • Research
  • Analysis
  • Design

Responsibilities

  • Competitive Benchmark
  • Usability Test
  • Affinity Diagram
  • Journey Map
  • Flow Diagram
  • Sketches
  • Prototype
  • Mockups
  • Developer Handoff Instructions

Research

The competitive benchmarking process mapped the current hotel booking landscape. It revealed established norms and potential trouble spots. It yielded a few key findings:

  • Some competitor checkout forms were quite lengthy
  • Some competitors interrupted the user flow with obtrustive sign-up calls to action
  • Date selection was often confusing

Insights were collected from three different usability tests. Two of the tests were part of the course materials, while the third was conducted live. Despite the small test group, usability testing allowed me to observe which parts of the process were positive and which sparked frustration. Users were quite communicative about what they liked and didn't like about the process:

"...puts me in the holiday mood"

- Tester 1, on a video of a swimming tropical fish

"...nearly put in the wrong dates"

- Tester 2, on struggles with a competitor's slow-loading calendar

"I have my contacts in, but I can't really read this"

- Tester 3, on encountering white mileage text over a light image

Analysis

On my first attempt at an affinity diagram, I created groups based on UI location. After re-working my diagram based on instructor feedback, new patterns emerged. Several things, like iconography and good copy, turned out to be overarching themes.

The customer journey map of the users' whole decision process showed that the beginning of the booking process is a high point, but complicated workflows and glitchy interfaces can really bring down the mood.

Design

The design phase involved planning the user's flow through the app as well as sketches of screens based on the insights gained in previous stages. I tried to minimize interactions to keep things simple for the user.

I paid special attention to issues such as:

  • Added a date selector with a vertical scroll to the next month to keep selection simple
  • Added a map to the top of the results page
  • Kept the checkout form as short as possible
  • Included TripAdvisor reviews widget to add trust

Conclusion

This project identified some key areas where a new hotel could outshine existing hotel apps. These areas included the search, calendar, and final booking stages.

"Nice prototype the CTA's were obvious. It was intuitive simple to use, well done. It's clear to see the solutions already defined in the flow diagram and interaction design have been depicted here."

Rebecca Bradley, UX Design Institute

Retrospective

  • Seeing someone else use an app was extremely helpful in thinking about improvements. People verbalizing frustration with a glitchy calendar or with low-contrast text were really valuable soundbites.
  • A survey would have added more helpful data and allowed for triangulation.
  • A user test of my working prototype would have provided data on whether moving the map and filters to the top of the screen helped users narrow down their searches faster.
